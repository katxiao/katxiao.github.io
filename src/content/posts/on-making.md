There is something that happens when your hands are covered in clay and nothing else exists except the weight of the material and the pressure of your fingers. No notifications. No cursor blinking. Just the slow, forgiving resistance of the earth giving way.

I have spent most of my working life making things on screens — designing systems, writing code, shaping ideas into products that other people will use. That kind of making is real. But it leaves no residue. When I close the laptop the work disappears, and so does the sense that I made anything at all.

## The permanence of objects

A bowl holds its shape after the fire. It does not need me to keep it open. There is something almost reckless about that — an object that exists independently of your continued attention.

> The things we make by hand carry the memory of their making in a way that digital objects do not.

I am still thinking about what that means for the other things I make. Whether there is a way to bring some of that quality — the residue, the trace of effort — into work that lives on a screen.

## Slowing down

One thing ceramics has taught me is that speed is not always a virtue. A pot thrown too fast collapses. You have to work with the material on its own terms, at its own pace. I find that surprisingly difficult for someone who has spent years optimising for throughput.
